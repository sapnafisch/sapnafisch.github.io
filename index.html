Engineer neurotechnologies the body won't reject — making advanced therapies safe, long-lasting, and truly translatable.

I am a Schmidt Science Fellow in the Boyden Lab at MIT, where I pioneered immune-tolerant optogenetic technologies that now enable safe, year-long control of peripheral nerves in preclinical models. Drawing on my earlier expertise in precision nanomaterial engineering and high-resolution TEM from Oxford, I converge experimental immunoengineering, computational protein design, and rational biological engineering to solve the central limitation of modern biotechnologies: the host immune response. My work makes durable optogenetic cell therapies for various applications.

Transforming the immune system from medicine's greatest obstacle into a predictable design parameter.

Today, clinicians manage immune rejection by suppressing the entire immune system — a blunt instrument with serious risks. My vision is a future where every advanced biotherapy is engineered from the ground up to be immune-compatible: not tolerated despite the body, but welcomed by it. By building generalizable principles that span biologics, gene delivery vectors, and biomaterials, I aim to establish a new field of immune-informed design that makes breakthrough therapies available to every patient, not just those lucky enough to pass an immune screening.

Enabling powerful technologies to truly work in humans

Because no patient should be told "your immune system says no" to a life-changing therapy.

This work matters to me because my scientific path has always been about convergence — moving from atomically precise nanomaterials at Oxford to immune-informed neuroengineering at MIT — and I saw firsthand how immunity silently kills the most elegant solutions before they can help anyone. It matters to the world because hundreds of millions of people live with chronic pain, sensory loss, genetic diseases, and metabolic disorders that viral gene therapies, optogenetic neuromodulation, and nanomedicines could treat — yet neutralizing antibodies, foreign-body responses, and T-cell rejection currently block them for up to 50–60% of patients and prevent redosing in nearly everyone. By solving the immune barrier at the design stage, we stop wasting brilliant technologies and start delivering real, durable cures.
